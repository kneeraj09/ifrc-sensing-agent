Signal Aggregation Methodology

Each ingestion cycle fetches raw text from all configured sources and passes each item to Claude, which extracts one or more structured signals. A signal encodes five dimensions: location, commodity, signal type, urgency, and confidence (0–1).

Belief states are computed from the last 72 hours of signals using a weighted aggregation:

P50 ≈ Σ(confidence_i × veracity_i × decay_i) / Σ(veracity_i × decay_i)
veracity_i — source weight (0.40–0.95), reflecting institutional credibility
decay_i — exponential time decay; signals older than 48h carry <50% weight
P10 / P90 — lower/upper bounds derived from the confidence distribution of contributing signals
  • One IFRC GO emergency event (wt 0.95) contributes more than ~16 GDELT articles (wt 0.60 each)
  • Signals are deduplicated by source: the same article processed twice does not double-count
  • Belief states are cleared and recomputed fresh each cycle — they are not cumulative across cycles
  • The SCQA executive briefing is generated by Claude from the top 20 belief states and refreshed every 30 minutes
Demand Uncertainty Representation

The demand index is explicitly probabilistic. A single P50 value without its P10–P90 spread is not meaningful — always consider the width of the uncertainty band.

Spread (P90−P10)Interpretation
< 0.2Narrow — multiple sources agree, high confidence in central estimate
0.2 – 0.5Moderate — typical for 2–3 sources with mixed recency
> 0.5Wide — sparse or contradictory signals; plan for worst case (P90)
Historical Hazard Baseline Methodology

The hazard baseline is computed independently of the real-time signal pipeline. It is a static risk profile derived from historical GDACS event records:

Severity Index = Σ(red events × 3 + orange × 2 + green × 1)
Accumulated over 5 calendar years. Reflects cumulative exposure, not peak single-event severity.
GLIDE linkage: each GDACS event's GLIDE number is matched against the IFRC GO events table to determine whether a formal IFRC response was activated.
Allocation Methodology — Newsvendor Model

The allocation engine uses a stochastic newsvendor model, the standard operations-research framework for one-period inventory decisions under uncertain demand. The model is applied independently per region, then results are merged.

Q*_i = μ_i + σ_i × Φ⁻¹(CR)
Q*_i — optimal allocation quantity for location i
μ_i — P50 demand estimate from Agent 1 belief state
σ_i — demand standard deviation ≈ (P90 − P10) / 2.56
Φ⁻¹(CR) — inverse standard normal CDF at the critical ratio. Converts a probability into a z-score: how many σ above the mean to stock in order to meet demand with probability CR. This is the "safety stock multiplier".
CR — critical ratio, the ethical service-level target: 0.70 Speed → 0.85 Balanced → 0.90 Equity → 0.95 Conservative
Key values: Φ⁻¹(0.50)=0.00  ·  Φ⁻¹(0.70)=0.52  ·  Φ⁻¹(0.85)=1.04  ·  Φ⁻¹(0.90)=1.28  ·  Φ⁻¹(0.95)=1.64

After computing Q* per location, two constraints are applied:

  • Equity floor: every location receives at least its P10 estimate regardless of CR
  • Budget constraint: if Σ Q*_i > available stock, residuals above the floor are scaled down proportionally so the total does not exceed available stock

The five ethical scenarios represent five distinct values of CR (and one risk-adjusted variant), allowing coordinators to compare the tradeoffs — coverage breadth vs. shortfall protection vs. equity — before selecting and ratifying.

Human Oversight Design Principles

The system is designed as a decision-support tool. No stock moves without an explicit human ratification step.

PrincipleHow it is implemented
TransparencyEvery AI recommendation shows its inputs — belief states, demand estimates, signal sources — so the coordinator can audit the reasoning
ExplainabilityThe decision brief explicitly names the ethical tradeoffs, shortfall risks, and assumptions underlying the selected scenario
Override authorityCoordinators can override any allocation quantity; overrides are displayed prominently in the brief and audit trail
AuditabilityEvery ratified run is stored with scenario, overrides, rationale, and timestamp — a permanent record for accountability
Impartiality checkThe decision brief explicitly references the IFRC impartiality principle and flags locations where the scenario creates significant inequality
Known Limitations
  • Demand estimates are relative, not absolute — the P50 index reflects signal intensity, not a physical quantity (MT, units). Map to quantities using reference baselines or field estimates
  • Coverage gaps skew beliefs — regions with fewer media and NGO reports will show systematically lower demand signals even if need is high
  • 72-hour window is a design choice — slow-onset crises (drought, displacement) may be under-represented relative to acute events
  • GLIDE matching is partial — approximately 60–70% of major IFRC operations have a GLIDE number; smaller operations and field reports often lack one
  • Newsvendor is a single-period model — it does not account for resupply cycles, lead times, or dynamic reallocation; treat it as a planning input, not a logistics schedule
  • Claude extraction accuracy varies — unstructured field messages may produce lower-confidence extractions; always review the signal list behind a belief state before acting on it

The demand index is a RELATIVE score (0.0–1.0), not an absolute quantity.

It is a weighted average of signal confidence, adjusted for source veracity and recency of signals.

Source Veracity Weights

Each source is weighted by institutional credibility. IFRC GO (0.95) carries the highest trust as IFRC's own first-party operational data. Signals from GDACS or ReliefWeb carry nearly twice the weight of a GDELT media article. The demand index reflects this automatically — one IFRC GO field report outweighs a dozen media headlines.

Key Rules for Interpretation
  • Index is RELATIVE — compare locations to each other, not to a fixed threshold
  • Check the signal count — P50 of 0.8 from 1 signal is weaker than 0.6 from 8
  • Signals decay over 48 hours — recent signals always outweigh older ones
  • Human overrides take precedence — field officers can annotate belief states
  • SCQA briefing refreshes every 30 min — reflects the last full ingestion cycle
Baseline Layer Hazard Baseline — Historical Risk & Response 5-year GDACS disaster archive linked to IFRC GO operations via GLIDE numbers
What the Hazard Baseline Is

The Hazard Baseline is a static analytical layer — separate from the real-time signal pipeline — that provides historical context for each country. It answers: "How often does this country get hit, by what type of disaster, and did IFRC respond?"

It combines two data sets:

  • GDACS historical — 5 years of earthquake, flood, cyclone, drought, wildfire, and volcano events via the GDACS REST API, filtered to IFRC priority regions
  • IFRC GO operations — 2 years of emergency events and appeals from the IFRC GO platform, cross-referenced via GLIDE numbers
How to Build the Baseline (Two Steps)
  1. Go to the Hazard Baseline dashboard (/hazard)
  2. Click ⬇ Load GDACS History — fetches 5 years of Africa disaster events (~30s). Progress shows in the log panel below the button
  3. Once done, click 🔗 Link IFRC GO — fetches 2 years of IFRC GO emergency operations and matches them to GDACS events (~20s)
  4. Page reloads automatically — the country table populates with severity profiles and IFRC GO response coverage
  5. Run again periodically (monthly recommended) to capture new events

GLIDE Number — The Linking Key
GLIDE (Global IDEntifier) is a shared disaster reference code adopted across humanitarian systems. When a cyclone hits Mozambique, GDACS, IFRC GO, UN OCHA, and national civil protection all reference the same GLIDE: e.g. TC-2023-000056-MOZ. This is the primary key that links GDACS historical events to IFRC GO operations.
  • GLIDE coverage is highest for large-scale operations (Emergency Appeals, DREF activations)
  • Smaller events may lack a GLIDE on one side — the system falls back to iso3 country matching
  • The N GLIDE tag in the hazard table shows exact event-level matches (not just country)

Agent 2 Demand Aggregation & Network Shaping Turns incoming logistics requests into consolidated shipment proposals
How Requests Enter the System
  1. Field staff send a logistics request by email, WhatsApp, or manual form in the Demand dashboard
  2. Claude reads each message and extracts a structured request (origin, destination, commodity, quantity, deadline)

How Clustering Works

The agent groups pending requests by commodity class (food, medical, NFIs…) and destination. A cluster forms when 2 or more compatible requests share the same corridor. Claude then generates a plain-language consolidation proposal for each cluster.

  • Commodity class is normalised — "rice" and "food rations" both map to food
  • Destination matching is case-insensitive ("Juba" = "juba")
  • Requests with different units (MT vs. cartons) can still be clustered if compatible
  • Clusters are rebuilt fresh each time the demand cycle runs — stale clusters are cleared first
Consolidation Proposals

Each proposal contains:

ElementWhat it tells you

RationaleWhy these requests can be combined (same corridor, commodity, timing)
Estimated savingApproximate cost or effort reduction from combining shipments
Suggested timingRecommended dispatch window based on the earliest deadline

Reviewing a Proposal
  1. Accept — marks the cluster as accepted; constituent requests move to accepted status
  2. Decline — dismisses the proposal; requests remain pending for future cycles
  3. Modify — accept with coordinator notes that override or qualify the AI recommendation

Manual requests always have confidence 1.0 and override extracted requests in ranking. Add coordinator notes to any proposal to create an audit trail for the decision.

When adding a stock position, assign it to a region. The newsvendor model runs independently per region, then results are merged. A location with no matching regional stock pool will not appear in the allocation output.

The Newsvendor Algorithm

The agent uses a multi-location newsvendor model to compute how to allocate a fixed regional stock across multiple locations under demand uncertainty. It uses the P10/P50/P90 demand estimates from Agent 1 as inputs.

Optimal quantity per location: Q* = μ + σ × Φ⁻¹(critical ratio)

  • μ = P50 demand estimate (the median signal from Agent 1)
  • σ = demand spread ≈ (P90 − P10) ÷ 2.56 (maps the 80% interval to standard deviations)
  • Φ⁻¹ = inverse standard normal CDF — converts a probability into a z-score (how many σ above the mean you must stock to cover demand with that probability). Example: Φ⁻¹(0.85) ≈ 1.04, so an 85% service level adds 1.04 × σ as safety stock above the median
  • Critical ratio = the ethical stance on acceptable risk of shortfall — higher means fewer shortfalls but uses more stock per location
  • An equity floor guarantees every location receives at least its P10 estimate regardless of CR
  • If total Q* exceeds regional stock, residuals above the floor are scaled down proportionally

Coordinator Workflow
  1. Go to the Allocation dashboard and add your stock position(s) — set the Region for each depot entry
  2. Enter the commodity and unit you want to allocate, then click Run allocation
  3. The system groups stock and belief states by region and runs the newsvendor model independently per region
  4. All 5 scenarios are computed simultaneously — compare the scenario cards: equity score, average coverage, locations at risk
  5. Click a scenario card to select it — Claude generates a 3-paragraph decision brief
  6. Optionally enter manual overrides for specific locations (your field judgement takes precedence)
  7. Review the brief: Situation, Ethical Rationale, Residual Risk
  8. Ratify the decision with a brief justification — this locks the run and creates an audit record
The Decision Brief

Claude generates a 3-paragraph brief for the selected scenario:

ParagraphContent
SituationWhat is being allocated, to whom, and under which scenario
Ethical RationaleTradeoffs made explicit — who benefits most, who is at risk of shortfall, alignment with the impartiality principle
Residual RiskWhat could go wrong, assumptions being made, what to monitor — flags any location with > 30% shortfall probability

The brief ends with one sentence the coordinator can use to justify the decision to stakeholders. Coordinator overrides are flagged explicitly in the brief.

Coordinator Overrides
  • Enter a specific quantity for any location in the override fields after selecting a scenario
  • Overrides are visible in the decision brief and audit trail — they are not hidden
  • If an override reduces a location's allocation below its equity floor, the brief will flag this
  • Use overrides when you have field intelligence the system cannot access (e.g. access route closed, stock already pre-positioned)
Key Principles
  • The algorithm is a decision support tool, not a decision-maker — the coordinator always ratifies
  • Stock is regionally pooled — enter a Region for every depot to ensure correct scoping
  • Allocation uses demand estimates from Agent 1 — run ingestion first for current data
  • A ratified run is locked; a new run must be started to revise an allocation
  • All runs are stored — the run history is accessible at the bottom of the Allocation dashboard
  • No allocation is distributed until explicitly ratified by a coordinator
Agent 4 Routing & Execution Orchestration Route graph · Composite scoring · Mission tracking · Disruption alerts
How Route Plans Are Built
  1. A coordinator ratifies an allocation run (Agent 3) — this triggers the route planner automatically
  2. The planner resolves the origin node from the stock position's depot location (matched against named waypoints)
  3. Each allocation destination becomes a separate candidate route plan
  4. The route graph runs a modified Dijkstra to find the highest-scoring path between origin and destination
  5. OpenRouteService (real road network via OSM) provides actual distance and drive time; haversine × 1.4 is used as fallback if no API key is set
  6. Plans are stored with status proposed — a coordinator must approve before a mission is activated
Route Composite Score

The planner maximises a composite score — it does not simply minimise distance.

Score = 0.50 × min_safety + 0.30 × prod_reliability − 0.20 × norm_time
min_safety — the lowest safety score of any segment on the path (weakest-link principle; one dangerous segment poisons the whole route)
prod_reliability — product of all segment reliability scores (reflects the compounding probability that every leg completes without disruption)
norm_time — estimated travel time normalised 0–1 across candidate paths; longer routes penalised but with lower weight than safety

Safety0.50ACLED conflict density + GDACS hazards + manual input
Reliability0.30Same three-layer prior (see below)
Time0.20ORS road distance ÷ 50 km/h convoy speed
Segment Safety & Reliability Scoring

Each route segment score is derived from a three-layer prior:

LayerWeightHow it is computed
ACLED conflict40%Events within 150 km of the segment midpoint, decayed with a 30-day half-life; urgency-weighted (immediate=1.0 → low=0.2)
GDACS hazard30%Active disaster alerts near the route from the hazard baseline; tier penalties: critical −0.45, high −0.30, medium −0.15, low −0.05
Coordinator input30%Manual reliability / safety override entered on the Routing dashboard; defaults to 0.75 if no data

All scores are clamped to [0.05, 0.99]. A segment is never rated zero or perfect — uncertainty is always present. Auto-generated edges (no manual data, no recent signals) default to 0.75.

Once a route plan is activated, a mission record is created and the monitor runs after each sensing cycle to check for problems.

  • Disruptions can be manually logged by coordinators and resolved once cleared

Coordinator Workflow — Routing
  1. Ratify an allocation run (Agent 3) — the planner runs automatically in the background
  2. Go to the Routing dashboard → Route Plans tab — proposed plans appear with path, distance, travel time, and composite score
  3. Review the score bar and path labels; check the map polyline for geographic sense
  4. Click Approve on plans that look operationally sound
  5. When a convoy is ready to depart, click Activate Mission — enter vehicle info, driver, estimated departure
  6. Field team logs Check-ins during transit; the monitor flags any silence > 6 h
  7. On arrival, click Complete Mission — the record is closed and archived
  8. If a disruption is detected, log it on the Disruptions tab and resolve it once cleared
Key Principles
  • The planner proposes — no convoy moves without a coordinator approving the plan and activating the mission
  • Safety is the primary routing criterion (weight 0.50) — a shorter but riskier route will lose to a longer safe one
  • Scores update automatically when Agent 1 ingests new conflict or hazard data — re-run the planner to reflect current conditions
  • Depot location in the stock position must match a waypoint name (e.g. "Nairobi", "Kampala") for the planner to resolve the origin
  • All routing decisions, approvals, check-ins, and disruptions are stored permanently in the database
